# Wilbur Content System Architecture

A senior curriculum architecture for Wilbur: what lessons exist, where they come from, how they’re simplified, and how they’re presented. For product and content teams. Architecture only—no full lesson copy.

---

## Product Context (Anchor)

**Wilbur is not:** A financial encyclopedia, a brokerage, or an advisor.

**Wilbur is:** The “Start Here” button for adult money.

**Users:** Beginners, overwhelmed, no finance background, mixed life stages.

**We do not want:** Overly technical explanations, regulatory deep dives, long academic articles, jargon-heavy definitions.

**We do want:** Simple explanations, practical understanding, real-life relevance, engaging structure, short lessons (5–8 min).

**Source ecosystem:** Government (IRS, CFPB, SEC, FDIC, Dept of Ed), Investopedia, other high-quality educational sources—then simplified, rewritten in Wilbur voice, structured into focused mini-lessons, and filtered for relevance.

# PART 1 — INTELLIGENT DOMAIN DISCOVERY

Domains, subtopics, and what becomes a full lesson vs glossary vs optional deep dive.

---

## Domain 1: Money Foundations

**Why it matters for beginners:** First place to start when they don’t know where to start. Builds mindset and one simple system.

**Core lessons (6–10):**
1. Why money habits matter now  
2. What is a budget (and why it’s not punishment)  
3. Your money in 3 buckets (spend / save / later)  
4. How much to save from every paycheck  
5. Emergency fund: how much and where  
6. Saving for short-term vs long-term goals  
7. Where to start when you’re behind  
8. One simple plan: automate and forget  

**Glossary-only terms:** 50/30/20, zero-based budget, pay-yourself-first, sinking fund, liquidity (mention in-context only), cash flow (in-context), net worth (glossary), asset (glossary), liability (glossary).

**Optional advanced:** “Advanced budgeting methods,” “Building a full financial plan.” Not in v1.

---

## Domain 2: Banking & Accounts

**Why it matters:** Where money lives. First account is a major milestone; confusion here blocks everything else.

**Core lessons (6–10):**
1. Checking vs savings: what’s the difference  
2. How to open your first account  
3. What is a high-yield savings account  
4. How to keep your money safe (scams and fraud)  
5. Your first paycheck: what’s taken out and why  

**Glossary-only terms:** ACH, overdraft, APY, FDIC (one sentence in lesson; full entry in glossary), routing number, direct deposit, minimum balance.

**Optional advanced:** “Types of bank accounts (money market, CD),” “How to switch banks.” Glossary or future short lesson.

---

## Domain 3: Credit & Debt Basics

**Why it matters:** Credit score and cards affect jobs, housing, and loans. Behavioral side (why people overspend) is as important as mechanics.

**Core lessons (6–10):**
1. What is a credit score (and why it’s not scary)  
2. How to check your credit (free and safe)  
3. Debit vs credit: when to use which  
4. How to use a credit card without debt  
5. Credit cards: when they help vs hurt  
6. Good debt vs bad debt (simple framing)  
7. How interest works when you owe money  
8. Building and protecting your credit score  
9. Credit cards: how to stop the cycle (behavioral)  

**Glossary-only terms:** FICO, VantageScore, utilization ratio, grace period, minimum payment, APR (define in lesson; glossary for reference), credit report, hard inquiry, soft inquiry, charge-off.

**Optional advanced:** “Disputing errors,” “Credit counseling vs debt settlement.” Glossary or single “when to get help” lesson.

---

## Domain 4: Investing — Stocks & Markets

**Why it matters:** Highest interest area. Must be broken into focused lessons so it’s not one overwhelming “Investing 101.”

**Core lessons (6–10):**
1. What is a stock (plain English)  
2. Why do stock prices change  
3. What is diversification  
4. What is an index (and why it matters)  
5. Index funds and ETFs in plain English  
6. What is risk and volatility (for beginners)  
7. What is a 401(k) (and why the match is free money)  
8. What is an IRA (simple version)  
9. How much to save for retirement (simple targets)  

**Glossary-only terms:** Dividend, P/E ratio, market cap, ticker, exchange, bull/bear, bond (separate lesson later or glossary), expense ratio (in ETF lesson; glossary), rebalancing, vesting (in 401(k); glossary).

**Optional advanced:** “Types of IRAs (Traditional vs Roth in depth),” “Target-date funds,” “Tax implications of selling.” Later modules.

---

## Domain 5: Crypto Basics

**Why it matters:** Curiosity is high; risk is high. Beginner-first, risk-aware, no “get rich” framing.

**Core lessons (5–7):**
1. What is cryptocurrency (plain English)  
2. Crypto vs stocks: what’s the difference  
3. Why crypto is riskier than stocks  
4. If you’re curious about crypto (what to know first)  
5. Stablecoins and DeFi: just the basics  

**Glossary-only terms:** Blockchain (one sentence in lesson), wallet, exchange (crypto), gas fee, NFT (one line), cold storage, hot wallet.

**Optional advanced:** “How to research a crypto project,” “Taxes on crypto.” Optional deep dives, not core path.

## Domain 6: Real Estate & Housing

**Why it matters:** Renting vs buying and “how much house” are top questions. Education-only; no “get rich in real estate.”

**Core lessons (6–9):**
1. Renting vs buying: what to consider

**Optional advanced:** “Refinancing when and why,” “Landlord basics.” Optional or future.

## Domain 7: Debt & Loans (Strategy)

**Why it matters:** Student loans and payoff strategy are make-or-break for many. Strategy framing, not product sales.

**Core lessons (6–8):**

**Glossary-only terms:** Principal, forbearance, deferment, income-driven repayment, forgiveness (PSLF, etc.—one line in lesson; glossary for types), consolidation, subsidized vs unsubsidized (in lesson; glossary).

**Optional advanced:** “Public Service Loan Forgiveness in depth,” “Private vs federal loans.” Deep dives or state-specific later.

## Domain 8: Retirement & Long-Term

7. Estate basics: why a will and beneficiaries matter  

**Glossary-only terms:** Vesting, employer match (in lesson; glossary), Traditional vs Roth (in lesson; glossary), required minimum distribution (RMD), beneficiary, will, trust (one line).

**Optional advanced:** “Roth conversion,” “HSA as retirement tool.” Not v1.

# PART 2 — SOURCE MAPPING

For each lesson type, what to pull from, what to use, and what to drop.

---

## Source types and roles

| Source type | Best for | Trust reason | Simplify because |
|-------------|----------|--------------|------------------|
| **IRS** | Tax withholding, W-4, refunds, filing | Authoritative, legal accuracy | Dense, form-oriented, multiple edge cases |
| **CFPB** | Credit, debt, mortgages, complaints | Consumer protection focus, plain-language efforts | Still long; need to cut to one idea per lesson |
| **SEC / Investor.gov** | Stocks, investing, fraud | Regulatory authority | Legal and technical language |
| **FDIC** | Bank accounts, safety, insurance limits | Authoritative on banks | Detail on limits and rules; we need one sentence |
| **Dept of Education / StudentAid** | Student loans, repayment, forgiveness | Official for federal loans | Forms and programs; we need “what it means” not “how to apply” |
| **Investopedia** | Definitions, concepts, comparisons | Broad, structured | Jargon, length, encyclopedic tone |
| **NerdWallet / similar** | Product comparisons, “how to” | Practical, readable | Often product-led; we take structure and simplify, no product push |

---

## Sourcing logic by domain

**Money Foundations**
- **Primary:** CFPB (budgeting, saving), behavioral research (habits).  
- **Use:** Core concepts (budget = plan, emergency fund size, automation).  
- **Ignore:** Multiple budgeting methods, studies citations, regulatory footnotes.  
- **Rewrite:** All into Wilbur voice; short paragraphs and one idea per block.

**Banking & Accounts**
- **Primary:** FDIC (safety, insurance), CFPB (account types, overdraft).  
- **Use:** “What is insured,” “what to look for when opening an account.”  
- **Ignore:** Exact coverage limits by account type, bank examination details.  
- **Rewrite:** “Your money is protected up to $250,000 per bank” → one sentence in lesson; link or glossary for details.

**Credit & Debt**
- **Primary:** CFPB (credit reports, scores, cards, debt collection), Experian/Equifax/TransUnion (how to get reports).  
- **Use:** What a score is, how to get it free, what helps/hurts, payoff strategies.  
- **Ignore:** FICO vs VantageScore nuance, exact formula, state debt-collection rules.  
- **Rewrite:** Any “consumers may” / “under the FCRA” into “You can…” and “Here’s what matters.”

**Investing — Stocks & Markets**
- **Primary:** SEC / Investor.gov (stocks, diversification, index funds, risk), Investopedia (definitions).  
- **Use:** Definitions, why prices move, what diversification is, what an index/ETF is.  
- **Ignore:** Order types, market microstructure, regulatory history.  
- **Rewrite:** All definitions into one-sentence plain English + one example.

**Crypto**
- **Primary:** SEC (risk, fraud), CFPB (warnings), one neutral explainer (what is blockchain in one sentence).  
- **Use:** “What it is,” “why it’s riskier,” “what to know if you’re curious.”  
- **Ignore:** Technical consensus, mining, layer-2, tokenomics.  
- **Rewrite:** No “revolution” or “future of money”; education and risk first.

**Real Estate & Housing**
- **Primary:** CFPB (mortgage shopping, closing), HUD (affordability), Investopedia (mortgage types, PMI).  
- **Use:** Affordability rules of thumb, fixed vs ARM, what PMI is, what closing costs are.  
- **Ignore:** State-specific licensing, full TILA/RESPA breakdown.  
- **Rewrite:** “Good faith estimate” etc. → “Here’s what you’ll pay at closing.”

**Debt & Loans (Student)**
- **Primary:** StudentAid.gov (loan types, repayment, forgiveness).  
- **Use:** Types in plain English, repayment options, “pay off vs invest” tradeoff.  
- **Ignore:** Full PSLF eligibility matrix, every IDR plan detail.  
- **Rewrite:** One table or list of “four ways to repay” with one line each; link to official for full rules.

**Retirement**
- **Primary:** IRS (401(k), IRA rules at high level), DOL (401(k) basics), Investopedia (definitions).  
- **Use:** What a 401(k)/IRA is, why match matters, simple “how much” targets.  
- **Ignore:** RMD tables, catch-up details, rollover rules in full.  
- **Rewrite:** “Tax-advantaged” → “You don’t pay tax on this money until later (or ever, for Roth).”

**Tax Basics**
- **Primary:** IRS (withholding, W-4, refund).  
- **Use:** What’s on a paycheck, what W-4 does, why you get a refund or owe.  
- **Ignore:** Form line-by-line, phase-outs, AMT.  
- **Rewrite:** “Adjust your withholding” → “Change how much is taken out so you’re not surprised at tax time.”

**Insurance**
- **Primary:** CMS/Healthcare.gov (health terms), NAIC or state ins dept (basic types).  
- **Use:** Deductible, premium, copay, network in one sentence each.  
- **Ignore:** Plan comparison details, state mandates.  
- **Rewrite:** All into “What you pay” and “What the plan pays.”

---

## Per-lesson sourcing (pattern)

For **any** lesson:

1. **Assign primary source(s)** by topic (e.g. “What is a stock” → SEC/Investor.gov + Investopedia definition).  
2. **Extract:** One definition, one “why it matters,” one “how it works” (3–5 steps or one mechanism).  
3. **Drop:** Second-level “why,” regulatory citations, edge cases, multiple methods unless we explicitly teach “two ways.”  
4. **Rewrite:** Every sentence into Wilbur voice (short, “you,” plain words).  
5. **Cite:** One “Learn more” link to primary source; no long footnotes in body.

# PART 3 — LESSON PRESENTATION STRUCTURE

Standard layout and domain-specific visuals.

---

## Standard lesson page layout

| Section | Purpose | Content rule |
|---------|--------|---------------|
| **Hero** | Identity and time | Title, 1-line subtitle, estimated time (e.g. 5–8 min), optional 1–3 hero takeaways |
| **Hook** | Why this matters now | 1–2 short paragraphs or 1 scenario/question |
| **Concept** | Core idea | Plain-language definition; 1 mental model or analogy |
| **Why it matters** | Real-life relevance | 2–3 bullets or 1 short paragraph |
| **How it works** | Mechanism | 3–5 steps or 1 simple flow; bullets or numbered list |
| **Visual** | See it | One chart/diagram/table (see below by domain) |
| **Common mistakes** | Pitfalls | 1–2 mistakes or myths; optional callout |
| **Mini scenario** | Make it real | One “Person A” or “You…” example (2–3 sentences) |
| **3 key takeaways** | Lock it in | 3 bullets, repeatable |
| **3-question quiz** | Check understanding | 3 MC questions; explanations in Wilbur voice |

**Length:** Total ~400–700 words of body (excluding quiz). One idea per section; no wall of text.

---

## Visuals by domain

| Domain | Best visual types | Example use |
|--------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Money Foundations** | Simple flow (buckets), timeline (savings over time) | 3 buckets diagram; “$50/month for 12 months” bar or line |
| **Banking** | Comparison table (checking vs savings), simple flow | Two-column “Use for” / “Keep for” |
| **Credit & Debt** | Timeline (payoff), comparison (avalanche vs snowball), simple flow | “Balance over time” with two lines (min payment vs extra); payoff order diagram |
| **Investing — Stocks** | Line chart (hypothetical price), pie (diversification), bar (growth over time) | “How a stock might move”; “One stock vs many”; “$100 over 10 years” |
| **Crypto** | Comparison table (crypto vs stocks), risk ladder or simple “risk dial” | Two-column; “Higher risk” callout |
| **Real Estate** | Comparison table (rent vs buy), bar or breakdown (closing costs), timeline (mortgage payoff) | “Costs of buying”; “Where the down payment goes” |
| **Debt & Loans** | Table (repayment options), timeline (avalanche vs snowball payoff) | “4 ways to repay student loans”; payoff curves |
| **Retirement** | Bar or line (match = free money), simple growth curve | “If you put in $X, match adds $Y”; “Growth over 20 years” |
| **Tax** | Paycheck breakdown (pie or bar), flow (withholding → refund/owe) | “Where your paycheck goes”; “Why you got a refund” |
| **Insurance** | Comparison table (plan types), flow (you pay X, plan pays Y) | “Deductible vs copay”; “What you pay vs what’s covered” |

---

## Interactivity

- **Quiz:** Always at end; 3 questions; immediate feedback with short explanation.  
- **Highlight explain:** Glossary terms and key phrases tappable; AI or glossary-first explain (no advice).  
- **Optional:** “Try it” calculator (e.g. payoff date, match calculator) as separate component; not required in every lesson.  
- **Progress:** “Mark complete” and “Next lesson” so the path is clear.

# PART 4 — RELEVANCE FILTERING SYSTEM

When to show a lesson, to whom, and what level it is.

---

## Level definitions

| Level | Definition | Example |
|-------|------------|---------|
| **Beginner** | No prior knowledge needed. First time seeing the topic. | What is a stock, What is a budget, What is a credit score |
| **Beginner+** | One or two prior lessons or life experience. Still no finance training. | Index funds (after “What is a stock”), IRA (after 401(k)), Pay off vs invest |
| **Intermediate** | Builds on several lessons or real-world experience. More tradeoffs, fewer “what is.” | Refinancing when and why, Tax-loss harvesting (if we add), STR and regulations |

---

## When a user sees a lesson

- **By life stage track:** User picks a track; they see 8–12 lessons in a fixed order. Same lesson can appear in multiple tracks in different positions.  
- **By domain browse:** User opens a domain; they see 5–10 lessons. Order can be “recommended” (prereqs first) or sequential.  
- **By search or “Learn more”:** Lesson appears in results or as related link. No forced order, but we show “Recommended first” if there’s a prereq.

---

## Prerequisites (simple)

- **None:** Money Foundations, Banking, “What is a stock,” “What is a credit score,” “What is a budget.”  
- **One lesson:** Most Beginner+ (e.g. “Index funds” after “What is a stock” or “What is an index”).  
- **Two or more:** “Pay debt vs invest” (debt + investing basics), “How much house” (budget + credit), “Real estate as investment” (stocks + diversification).

Stored as: `prereq_lesson_ids: string[]` (optional). UI: “Recommended first: [Lesson A]” if not completed.

---

## When to hide (prevent overwhelm)

- **Don’t show full library as default.** Show one track or one domain.  
- **Don’t show Intermediate in “Start here” or “First job” track.** Intermediate appears in “Catching up” or domain browse.  
- **Optional deep dives** are not in main track lists; they’re “More in this topic” or “Advanced” at bottom of domain.  
- **State-specific or year-specific (e.g. tax law)** can be a separate “State guides” or “Updates” area so core library stays stable.

---

## Life stage ↔ lesson mapping (summary)

Same as in **Wilbur Content Architecture**: each track has an ordered list of lesson slugs. A lesson can be in multiple tracks. Filtering = “User is on Track X” → show only that track’s list in order; “User is in Domain Y” → show that domain’s lessons (with optional “Start with” hint).

# PART 5 — SIMPLIFICATION RULES

How we turn long, technical source material into short Wilbur lessons.

---

## Rules

1. **One idea per paragraph.** Max 2–3 sentences per paragraph.  
2. **One definition per term.** First use = one sentence in plain English + one example if needed.  
3. **Cut regulatory attribution.** No “Under the FCRA…” or “The IRS states….” Say “You can…” or “Here’s how it works.”  
4. **Cut edge cases.** If it applies to &lt;10% of beginners, drop it or one line + “Learn more” link.  
5. **Replace jargon with plain language.** Use the Wilbur Content System jargon table (APR, diversification, etc.).  
6. **Turn long articles into one lesson.** 2,000-word source → 500–700 words: one hook, one concept, one “how it works,” one visual, pitfalls, scenario, takeaways.  
7. **Tables and lists over prose.** “Four repayment options” → table with one line each, not four paragraphs.  
8. **Passive → active, “one” → “you.”** “Investors may consider” → “You might think about.”

---

## Target length by source type

| Source type | Typical length | Wilbur target | Strategy |
|-------------|----------------|---------------|----------|
| Investopedia article | 1,500–3,000 words | 500–600 words | Keep one definition, one “why,” one “how”; drop history, variants, related terms |
| IRS page | 800–2,000 words | 400–500 words | One “what you need to know”; link to form or tool |
| CFPB explainer | 1,000–2,500 words | 500–600 words | One key message; turn steps into bullets; one example |
| SEC/Investor.gov | 1,000–2,000 words | 500–600 words | Definition + risk in plain language; drop legal citations |

---

## Example rewrites

**Investopedia-style (before):**

> “Dollar-cost averaging (DCA) is an investment strategy in which an investor divides up the total amount to be invested across periodic purchases of a target asset in an effort to reduce the impact of volatility on the overall purchase. The purchases occur regardless of the asset’s price and at regular intervals. In effect, this strategy removes much of the detailed work of attempting to time the market in order to make purchases of equities at the most favorable prices.”

**Wilbur-style (after):**

> When you invest a fixed amount on a regular schedule—say $50 every month—you buy more shares when prices are low and fewer when they’re high. That can smooth out the effect of ups and downs over time. You don’t have to guess the “right” time to buy.

---

**IRS-style (before):**

> “Your withholding is the amount of federal income tax withheld from your paycheck by your employer. The amount withheld is based on the information you provide on Form W-4 and the IRS withholding tables. If too much tax is withheld, you may receive a refund when you file your return; if too little is withheld, you may owe tax when you file.”

**Wilbur-style (after):**

> When you start a job, you fill out a form (W-4) that tells your employer how much tax to take from each paycheck. If they take too much, you get money back at tax time (a refund). If they take too little, you owe. You can change your W-4 so the amount taken out fits your situation better.

# PART 6 — SCALABILITY SYSTEM

How we add sources, topics, and regions without bloat.

---

## Adding new trusted sources

1. **Classify source:** Government (which agency), editorial (Investopedia, etc.), or other.  
2. **Map to domains:** Which domain(s) does this source cover? Attach to those domains in the CMS or content repo.  
3. **Tag content type:** “Definition,” “How-to,” “Warning,” “Comparison.” Use when picking which lesson to update.  
4. **Extract and simplify:** Use Part 5 rules. New source doesn’t mean new lesson; it often means “update or enrich existing lesson X.”  
5. **Cite:** Add “Source: [Name]” and link in lesson footer or “Learn more.”

**Rule:** Prefer updating existing lessons with better or newer sources over adding new lessons.

---

## Adding new topics (e.g. AI investing, new tax laws)

1. **Fit check:** Does it fit a current domain or a new one? If new, do we need a new domain or a “Light” domain (like Tax, Insurance)?  
2. **Beginner test:** Can we explain it in 5–8 minutes at Beginner level? If not, it’s glossary or optional deep dive first.  
3. **One lesson first:** Add one “What is X” or “X in plain English” lesson. Don’t add 5 lessons at once.  
4. **Life stage and prereqs:** Which track(s) and what prerequisites? Add to track list and prereq field.  
5. **Review balance:** After adding, count lessons per domain. If any domain goes above 10 lessons, consider merging or moving one to “Optional.”

**Rule:** New topic = one new lesson (or one new “Light” domain with 3–4 lessons). Expand only after that’s live and validated.

---

## Adding state guides

1. **Template:** One “State guide” content type: state name, topic (e.g. “State tax,” “State aid”), 3–5 sections (what’s different, where to go, one link to official).  
2. **Separate from core lessons:** State guides live under “Guides” or “By state,” not mixed into “Stocks” or “Tax.”  
3. **Reuse core lesson:** State guide can say “After reading [Tax basics lesson], here’s what’s different in California.”  
4. **Maintainability:** One owner or process for “update state guides when laws change.” Core library stays stable.

**Rule:** State-specific = state guide, not 50 new lessons in the core library.

---

## Updating content without overwhelming the library

1. **Version or “Updated” date:** Store `updated_at` or “Last updated” so we can show it and audit.  
2. **Change log (internal):** Short note per lesson: “Source refreshed from CFPB 2024”; “Simplified paragraph 2.”  
3. **No “What’s new” sprawl:** Don’t add a “New” badge to 20 lessons. Add to 1–2 and feature in onboarding or home.  
4. **Retire with care:** If we remove a lesson, redirect to the best replacement and remove from all track lists.

**Rule:** Updates are in-place edits and optional “Last updated” + internal log. New lessons are rare and deliberate.

---

## Preventing category bloat

- **Cap per domain:** 10 lessons in core path. 11th lesson must be “Optional” or merged.  
- **Cap domains:** 10 domains. New area = “Light” (3–4 lessons) or merge into existing (e.g. “HSA” under Retirement or Tax).  
- **Glossary is the release valve:** When we want to “add” something but it’s not a full lesson, add a glossary entry and link from the relevant lesson.

---

## Maintaining balance across domains

- **Quarterly check:** Count lessons per domain. Flag any domain &gt;10 or &lt;4.  
- **Track coverage:** Each life stage track should have 8–12 lessons and touch at least 3 domains. If a track is all one domain, add one lesson from another domain (e.g. “When to get help” from Retirement into Debt track).  
- **Depth mix:** Per domain, aim 60–70% Beginner, 20–30% Beginner+, 10% Intermediate. Adjust when adding.
